Bio: I am a recent Master's graduate in Robotics from the Georgia Institute of Technology. During my time at Georgia Tech, my research focused on multi-agent optimization using self-supervised learning at the intersection of computer vision, motion planning, deep learning, and autonomous navigation. Currently, I work as an Autonomous Vehicle Engineer at Textron Systems and also serve as an Instructional Associate for the graduate-level AI course at Georgia Tech.

My passion of robotics ignited during my undergraduate studies at The Pennsylvania State University, where I majored in Mechanical Engineering. I spent two years conducting research at Basak Research Group, during which I worked on developing a novel apparatus to measure the metallic properties of materials. I spent another year with the Penn State Advanced Vehicle Team (AVT) as the simulation lead, converting an Electric Vehicle to an autonomous vehicle for GM’s AutoDrive II competition.

I am excited to continue my journey in the field of robotics and make meaningful contributions to the development of autonomous systems. In the future, I aim to build expertise in reinforcement learning and perception-based robotics, with a focus on developing adaptive, learned behaviors for autonomous systems.

Definitely reach out to me if you’d like to collaborate!

3D Point Cloud Classification
I explored the classification of 3D point clouds, a process essential for interpreting vast sets of three-dimensional data gathered from sensors like LiDAR. I evaluated various algorithms, each with its unique strengths and limitations. PointNet and its advanced version, PointNet++, specifically designed for point cloud data, directly processed points without needing transformations. PointNet was simple and efficient but less effective with dense clouds, while PointNet++ offered improved detail handling at the cost of increased complexity. Lastly, Graph-Based Neural Networks, treating point clouds as graphs, excelled in capturing local structures. Their performance, however, hinged on the quality of graph construction and came with high computational requirements. Balancing these factors was key in selecting the right approach for the project’s needs.
Code
Autonomous Search and rescue Robot
In my object detection project, I utilized the pre-trained YOLOv5 model from the Ultralytics repository to detect and classify emotions on human faces. Using the open-source software "labelme," I annotated images to create labeled datasets for training and validation. During the training phase, I fine-tuned the YOLOv5 model, optimizing its parameters to accurately identify "Happy" or "Neutral" expressions. Achieving high accuracy with the trained model, I then deployed the weights on an Android phone, developing a custom application for real-time emotion detection. This project showcases the practical application of object detection techniques for emotion recognition, with the ability to deploy such a solution on mobile devices.
